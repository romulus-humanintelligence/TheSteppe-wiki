    Cultures¶

    Four cultures inhabit the steppe world.

    Scythian¶

    They are called the Scoloti by their own tongues, though the Greeks name them Scythians. From the forests where the rivers run black to the endless grass sea of the Pontic shore, they ride. Born to the saddle before they walk, the Royal Scythians know no master save the sky-father Papaeus and the hooves that carry them. Their tents — felt and hide stretched over bent wood — move with the herds, with the seasons, with war. Gold glitters at their throats and wrists, hammered into the shapes of beasts: stag and griffin, wolf and eagle, coiled in eternal hunt. Their dead sleep beneath kurgans, the great mounds that rise from the steppe like the earth's own memory, surrounded by horses strangled in honor, weapons broken in sacrifice, and the skulls of enemies turned to drinking cups.

    The bow is their language. A Scythian child draws before speaking full sentences; by manhood the arrow and the eye are one. They shoot backward from the saddle at full gallop, they feather wolves in moonlight, they have turned Persian armies into carrion fields. Yet they are not only killers. They know the mare's milk fermented into kumis, sharp and sour; they know the hemp seeds cast upon hot stones in the low tents, the sacred vapor that cleanses and opens the inner eye. Their women ride, fight, and rule as the men do — Herodotus himself swore to it, though his countrymen scoff. Blood oaths bind them. Kin is steel. Betrayal earns the scalp-knife and the unmarked grave.

    The steppe is their mother, brutal and true. In winter the wind cuts like a blade; in summer the grass stands tall as a horse's belly, gold and green to the horizon. They follow no walls, plow no fields, bow to no king who does not earn his seat in war. The Greeks call them barbarians, but the Greeks cower behind stone while the Scythians ride free beneath the endless sky, their ancestors watching from the smoke of a thousand fires, their songs carried on the wind that never stops.

    Sarmatian¶

    East of the Scythians, where the grass grows harsher and the winters bite deeper, the Sarmatians hold the steppe. They call themselves the Sauromatae, the lizard-people, though there is nothing cold-blooded in their way of war. They are the iron riders, clad in scales of bronze and iron like the serpents of old myth, their lances longer than a man is tall, their horses barded in metal that glints like water under the steppe sun. Where the Scythian strikes from distance, the Sarmatian closes and crushes. Their charge is an avalanche of iron and flesh, unstoppable, shattering shield walls and scattering infantry like wheat before the thresher.

    Their women are not gentle. The Greeks whisper of Amazons, and they are not wrong — Sarmatian girls learn the sword before the loom, and no warrior may marry until she has slain an enemy in battle. Tombs of chieftains hold swords and armor beside the bones of women as often as men. The outsider sees madness in this; the Sarmatian sees only sense. The steppe does not care what flesh you wear. Only that you stand when the wolves come.

    They are the eastern wind, restless and fierce. Their forges burn hotter than the Scythians'; their iron is better, their armor heavier. They disdain gold for the weight of war-metal, and where the Scythian rides light and loose, the Sarmatian is a fist of iron driving into the enemy's heart. They honor the sword-god with oaths sworn on blades planted in the earth, and they keep those oaths until death. The steppe remembers them as the ones who feared nothing — not the Persians, not the Greeks, not even the bitter cold that kills horses standing. They endure.

    • Lifestyle: Nomadic
    • Settlements: 3-5
    • Preferred Biomes: Central Asian Steppe, Pontic Steppe

    Elder Names: Gatalos, Medosakkos, Pharzoios, Inismeos, Rasparagos

    Saka¶

    The Persians call them Saka, the archer-folk beyond the great river, but they are kin to the Scythians — the eastern branch, tempered by mountain winds and desert heat. They wear the pointed caps that mark them from afar, tall and proud as the peaks of the Tian Shan, and their bows sing songs that have felled kings. Ask the shade of Cyrus the Great, who brought an empire to the edge of the world and found Queen Tomyris waiting with her warriors, her son's blood still wet on Persian blades. She drowned Cyrus in a skin of blood and his army fed the crows. The Saka do not forgive. The Saka do not forget.

    They are shamans and sky-watchers, drinkers of haoma — the sacred plant that opens the doors between worlds, that grants visions of ancestors and animal spirits, that turns a man into something more than flesh when battle calls. Their gold rivals the Scythians' — the Golden Man of Issyk lies in his kurgan still, wrapped in four thousand pieces of hammered sun-metal, a god made manifest. But their wealth is not for display alone. It is offering, tribute to the spirits who ride the wind and dwell in the high places where the air grows thin and the eagles nest.

    The steppe here is different — harder, drier, edged by deserts that swallow the unwary and mountains that scrape the sky. The Saka know thirst and cold in equal measure, and they have learned to move like water, like wind, striking where the enemy is weakest and vanishing before the counterattack. Their arrows find their mark at distances that make other archers weep. They are the distant ones, the eastern shadows, the riders who learned to fight Persians and lived to tell the tale. The bow speaks louder than the tongue, they say, and the eastern winds carry their destiny onward, always onward, beneath the endless sky.

    Greek Colony¶

    Where the steppe meets the sea, where the cold waters of the Pontic shore lap against stone docks and timber wharves, the Greeks have planted their cities. Olbia, Panticapaeum, Chersonesus — names that taste of wine and olive oil, of marble columns rising improbably from the grass-sea's edge. They came as traders first, selling amphoras and buying grain, and they stayed because the steppelands are rich and the Black Sea is the highway to Athens, to Corinth, to the warm heart of the civilized world. They are not nomads. They do not ride. They build walls and write laws and honor the gods with proper temples, not smoky felt tents and horse-sacrifice.

    The Bosporan Kingdom rules them now, half-Greek and half-Scythian, a mongrel dynasty that speaks both tongues and honors both ways. Their kings take Scythian wives and Greek titles, command phalanxes and horse-archers alike, and grow fat on the grain trade that feeds distant cities. The colonists themselves are merchants and vintners, shipwrights and scribes, men who measure wealth in silver drachmas and amphorae of wine, not in horses and scalps. They look upon the steppe-riders with a mix of awe and disdain — barbarians, yes, but dangerous ones, and profitable if handled carefully.

    Yet they are not soft. The walls are strong, the hoplites drill in formation, and every colonist knows that the steppe is a breath away, that the tribes could sweep down and erase these fragile cities in a season if they chose. So the Greeks trade, and they negotiate, and they adapt. They learn to drink kumis without grimacing, to speak enough Scythian to bargain for furs and slaves, to commission animal-style goldwork for their own tombs. They bring civilization to the edge of the world, and the world changes them in return. The sea is their mother, but the steppe is at their door, and it watches with eyes that never blink.
